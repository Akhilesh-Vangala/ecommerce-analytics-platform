# Case Study: Olist E-Commerce Analytics — From Raw Marketplace Data to Operational & Customer Strategy

**Role simulated:** Data Analyst / Business Analyst supporting Operations, Customer
Experience, and Marketplace leadership at a large e-commerce/logistics company.
**Dataset:** [Olist Brazilian E-Commerce Public Dataset](https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/olistbr/brazilian-ecommerce)
— 99,441 orders, 96,096 customers, 3,095 sellers, 74 product categories, Sep 2016 –
Sep 2018.
**Stack:** PostgreSQL 16 (Redshift-compatible SQL) → Python (pandas, scikit-learn,
statsmodels, scipy) → Tableau Public.

---

## Executive Summary

This project takes a raw, multi-table e-commerce export and turns it into the kind of
analytics stack a Data Analyst would be expected to build and maintain: a layered SQL
warehouse with an automated data-quality framework, a 4-domain SQL query catalog
answering real business questions, six structured analysis notebooks (EDA → time
series → geospatial → hypothesis testing → segmentation → predictive risk modeling),
and a 7-page, 24-extract interactive Tableau dashboard.

**Headline numbers:**

- **R$15.74M GMV** across 99,441 orders (AOV **R$160.23**), Sep 2016 – Sep 2018
- **8.11% of orders arrive late**, but the late rate **varies 8x by state** (2.9% → 23.9%)
- **Orders delayed 4+ days are only 5.2% of reviewed deliveries but account for ~26% of
  all negative (1-2★) reviews** — the single most actionable finding in the project
  (see Recommendation 1)
- A 4-cluster behavioral segmentation found an **"At-Risk: Late & Unhappy"** segment
  (7,319 customers, 7.9% of the base) that is **invisible to standard RFM
  segmentation** (it shows up inside every RFM tier) and is 1.77x overrepresented in
  the Northeast
- Two production-style RandomForest models flag the riskiest 10% of orders at
  **order time**, catching **31.9% of all eventual late deliveries** (3.2x lift) and,
  post-delivery, the riskiest 10% of orders have a **56.0% negative-review rate**
  (4.4x the 12.8% baseline)

**Headline recommendations** (fully developed in Section 6): (1) proactive delay
communication for orders trending toward 4+ day delays, (2) order-time risk triage
using the late-delivery model's top decile, (3) Northeast-focused fulfillment
investment, (4) a win-back campaign for the "High-Value Lapsed" RFM segment (R$224K–
R$448K illustrative incremental GMV), (5) a loyalty program targeting the "Loyal
Repeat Customers" behavioral profile, (6) a seller-performance program addressing
marketplace concentration risk, (7) category-level product/expectation audits
independent of logistics, and (8) seasonal capacity planning ahead of November demand
spikes.

## 1. Business Context & Problem Framing

A marketplace like Olist (or, at the scale this project is designed to demonstrate
fluency for, Amazon) generates the same recurring questions for its analytics
function, regardless of size:

1. **Where is revenue coming from, and how is it trending?** (growth, seasonality,
   category mix, geographic concentration)
2. **Why are customers unhappy, and can we see it coming?** (delivery performance,
   review scores, the link between the two)
3. **Where does the fulfillment network break down, and why?** (cycle-time stages,
   regional variation, anomaly periods)
4. **Who are our best and most at-risk customers, and what should we do about each
   group?** (segmentation, lifecycle, win-back)
5. **Can we act *before* an order fails, instead of just measuring it afterward?**
   (predictive risk scoring)

Each of the 7 dashboard pages (and the notebook work behind it) maps directly to one
of these five questions. The project is deliberately scoped as "one analyst, full
stack, real findings, real recommendations" — the same shape of work a DA/BA is asked
to do end-to-end, just compressed into a portfolio timeline.

## 2. Data & Technical Architecture

- **Raw → Staging → Marts** layered warehouse in PostgreSQL 16, written in
  Redshift-compatible SQL (DISTKEY/SORTKEY choices documented as comments for a future
  Redshift migration).
- **Star schema marts**: `dim_date`, `dim_customer`, `dim_product`, `dim_seller`,
  `dim_geography`, `fact_orders`, `fact_order_items`, `fact_order_payments`.
- **Data quality framework**: 39 automated checks (completeness, uniqueness,
  referential integrity, business-rule assertions) — **32 PASS / 0 FAIL / 7 INFO**.
  Every INFO row documents a known, handled data reality (e.g., 547 orders with
  duplicate review rows, deduplicated to most-recent; 610 products with missing
  category, mapped to `"unknown"`).
- **24-CSV / 7-page Tableau extract layer**, generated by a single idempotent script
  (`etl/export_dashboard_extracts.py`) that re-derives every aggregate, cluster
  assignment, and risk score from the marts on each run — there is no hand-edited
  output anywhere in the pipeline.

Full architecture detail: see the Architecture section of `README.md`.

## 3. Methodology

**Phase 1 — SQL analytics catalog.** Four domains of business-question-driven SQL
(CTEs, window functions): revenue & growth, customer RFM/cohort retention, fulfillment
& SLA performance, seller/marketplace performance.

**Phase 2 — Exploratory & statistical analysis (Notebooks 1–4).**
- NB1: full data profiling — missingness, distributions, univariate/bivariate/
  multivariate EDA, correlation structure.
- NB2: time series decomposition (STL), stationarity testing (ADF), Holt-Winters
  forecasting.
- NB3: geospatial analysis — state and city-level demand, delivery performance, and
  supply/demand balance.
- NB4: formal hypothesis testing (Mann-Whitney, Kruskal-Wallis with Holm correction,
  chi-square/Cramér's V, Spearman) on the delay→review relationship and category/
  region effects, with effect sizes throughout (not just p-values).

**Phase 3 — Advanced analytics (Notebooks 5–6).**
- NB5: RFM quintile segmentation + a 10-feature K-Means (k=4, validated via
  elbow/silhouette) behavioral segmentation, visualized via PCA, validated for
  stability via bootstrap (mean ARI = 0.979).
- NB6: two RandomForest classifiers — "Model A" predicts late delivery **at order
  time** (no information not available when the order is placed); "Model B" predicts
  a negative review **using post-delivery information**. Both evaluated with
  AUC/PR-AUC, decile lift tables, and grouped feature importance.

**Phase 4 — Dashboard.** `export_dashboard_extracts.py` retrains both NB6 models on
the **full population** (the "production" framing — NB6's held-out metrics, AUC=0.742/
PR-AUC=0.215 for Model A, AUC≈0.745/PR-AUC≈0.270 for Model B, are the validation
evidence that the approach generalizes) and reproduces NB5's segmentation with
dynamic, characteristic-based segment naming (cluster IDs from K-Means are arbitrary
across reruns; segments are named by matching each cluster's defining trait — e.g.
the cluster with by far the highest repeat-purchase rate is always named "Loyal Repeat
Customers", regardless of which integer K-Means assigns it). Output: 24 CSVs feeding
a 7-page Tableau Public dashboard.

## 4. Key Findings

Findings are numbered #1–50 across the six notebooks (NB1=#1-14, NB2=#15-22,
NB3=#23-30, NB4=#31-36, NB5=#37-42, NB6=#44-50). Organized here by theme.

### 4.1 Data foundation
The star schema is **fully reconciled** — every mart row count matches its raw source
1:1, and every missingness pattern traces to an explainable lifecycle stage (no
imputation needed anywhere, #1). The order funnel is healthy: **97.02%** of orders
reach `delivered` (#2); non-delivered statuses are small and immaterial.

### 4.2 Growth, seasonality & forecasting
- **November 2017 is the dataset's one exceptional month**: GMV is **+64%** vs. its
  trailing 3-month average, independently corroborated by STL decomposition (+R$298,584
  seasonal term, ~34% of trend) (#18).
- The **mature period (Jun-2017–Jul-2018) grows ~R$45,955/month** (linear, R²=0.673)
  or **~5.84% compounding MoM** (R²=0.697), both significant at p<0.001 (#20).
- The apparent **Jun–Aug 2018 "decline" is substantially a right-censoring artifact**:
  Aug-2018 has only 29/31 days of data, and the final 5 observed days taper sharply
  (69→11 orders/day) from survivorship bias toward fast-delivered orders (#16). A
  triangulation across 3 methods (reported actual, Holt-Winters forecast, day-count
  extrapolation) places **true Aug-2018 GMV above the reported figure** — growth
  likely continued (#17).
- Day-of-week seasonality is strong: **Monday is the peak order day (114% of weekly
  average)**, decaying to a Saturday trough (77%) — a classic B2C "weekday browsing"
  pattern (#15).
- A Holt-Winters forecast projects **Sep–Nov 2018 GMV of ~R$1.0–1.05M**, with wide 95%
  CIs (±R$350K–R$525K) — directional only (#22).

### 4.3 Geographic concentration
- **Demand is heavily concentrated**: SP alone = **37.4% of GMV / 41.8% of
  customers**; the top 5 states (SP, RJ, MG, RS, PR) = **73.1% of GMV** (#23).
- **Seller supply is even more concentrated than demand**: SP supplies **64.6%** of
  item revenue but consumes only 37.4% — a self-sufficiency ratio of 1.73 (#29).
- **City-level concentration is sharper than state-level**: São Paulo city alone ≈
  13.7% of national GMV; the top 100 of 4,248 cities = 60% of GMV (#30).

### 4.4 Delivery performance & geography
- **Late-delivery rate varies 8x by state**: 2.9% (RO) to **23.9% (AL)**, with a clear
  Northeast cluster (AL, MA, PI, CE, SE, BA — all ≥14%) standing apart from the
  Southeast/South core (SP, MG, PR all ≤6%) (#24).
- It's not absolute delivery time that predicts lateness — it's **how thin the SLA
  buffer is** (r=0.72 between buffer thinness and `pct_late`). The Northeast cluster
  has the thinnest buffers (-8 to -10 days) and highest `pct_late`; deep-North states
  (AC, AP, AM, RO) have the *longest* buffers and lowest `pct_late` (#25).
- **64% of single-seller orders are fulfilled cross-state** — the majority case, not
  an edge case — taking ~1.9x longer (15.2 vs. 8.0 days) and costing ~75% more
  freight (#28).
- Order-level distance-decay is monotonic across 8 distance buckets: delivery time
  rises 3.7x, freight share rises 10.5%→18.5%, and `pct_late` more than doubles
  (5.8%→13.7%) (#27).
- Independently confirming this from the regional rollup built for this case study:
  **Northeast = 9.47% of orders but 14.33% late rate and 3.97★ avg review**, vs.
  Southeast (68.57% of orders, 7.45% late, 4.18★) and South (14.26% of orders, 7.05%
  late, 4.19★).

### 4.5 Delivery delay → customer satisfaction
- `review_score` is **J-shaped**: 59.9% are 5★, but **11.7% are 1★** — more than the
  2★+3★ combined for the bottom half of the distribution (#5).
- The typical order arrives **~12 days *ahead* of its estimate** (median delay = -11.9
  days) — SLA promises are generously padded (#6, #13).
- **`is_late` is the strongest review-score predictor tested**: Mann-Whitney p≈0,
  Cliff's delta = -0.554 ("large"); mean review falls **4.29 → 2.57** for late orders
  (#31).
- The delay→review relationship is a **cliff, not a slope**. All 10 pairwise
  delay-bucket comparisons remain significant after Holm correction, but the
  *magnitude* of the drop is concentrated in two buckets: avg review holds at
  **4.30 → 4.14 → 3.77** for Early → On-time → Late 1-3 days, then **falls off a cliff
  to 2.32 (Late 4-7) and 1.73 (Late 8+)** (#10, #32).
- **New for this case study** — translating that cliff into negative-review rates:
  Early=9.15%, On-time=11.40%, Late 1-3=19.12%, **Late 4-7=61.25%, Late 8+=78.44%**.
  The two "severely late" buckets are **only 5,025 of 95,824 reviewed deliveries
  (5.24%)** but account for **3,637 of 12,275 negative reviews — 29.6% of all of
  them**. If those 5,025 orders had instead landed at the "Early" baseline negative
  rate (9.15%), only ~460 would be negative — implying **~3,177 "excess" negative
  reviews (25.9% of all negative reviews) are attributable to 4+ day delays alone**.
  This is the single highest-leverage number in the project (→ Recommendation 1).
- Across every order-value decile, the Early→Late8+ swing is the **same ~2.5 stars**,
  with only a minor (~0.15–0.2 star) baseline-expectation effect for high-value
  orders — the delay penalty is structural, not value-dependent (#14).

### 4.6 Pricing, categories & payments
- All monetary variables are **extremely right-skewed** (skew 8-12, kurtosis
  120-560) — log1p transforms are required for any linear/logistic modeling (#4).
- Price↔freight correlation is only moderate (r≈0.41-0.43) — freight is driven more by
  weight/dimensions/distance than declared value, especially for "bulky" categories
  (#9).
- Installment count is a strong proxy for basket size: mean order value **triples**
  from R$119 (1 installment) to R$355 (11-24 installments) (#11).
- **16 of 53 product categories significantly differ from the grand-mean review score**
  (Holm-corrected) — `office_furniture` (3.51★) vs. `books_general_interest` (4.51★),
  a full 1-star gap **independent of delivery performance** (#33).
- Region has only a "negligible-to-small" effect on review score / delay (#34); payment
  type predicts order value but **not** review score — a deliberate demonstration that
  statistical significance ≠ practical significance at n≈96K (#35).

### 4.7 Customer segmentation
- RFM quintile segmentation: **Champions (16.5% of customers, 30.6% of revenue)**,
  Mid-Value (36.0% / 29.4%), **High-Value Lapsed (15.6% / 29.3%)**, Low-Value Lapsed
  (16.5% / 5.6%), New/Recent Low-Spend (15.4% / 5.1%) (#37).
- A separate **behavioral** clustering (k=4, validated via elbow/silhouette, PCA
  explains ~46% of variance: PC1=27.9%, PC2=18.5%) finds 4 segments defined by single
  dominant traits, not revenue alone (#38, #39):

| Segment | % of customers | % of revenue | Defining trait |
|---|---|---|---|
| Core High-Value | 44.5% | 69.5% | Highest per-customer monetary (R$257.80) and lowest delay/late rates |
| Budget Satisfied | 45.1% | 17.7% | Low ticket (R$64.71), high satisfaction (4.33★), 1 category |
| **At-Risk: Late & Unhappy** | **7.9%** | 8.3% | **99.7% of orders late**, avg delay **+9.5 days** (the *only* segment with positive avg delay), avg review **2.55★** |
| Loyal Repeat Customers | 2.5% | 4.6% | **71.5% repeat-purchase rate** (vs. ≤1.78% everywhere else), 1.85 avg frequency, 2.05 categories, 3.68 installments |

- **The At-Risk segment exists inside every RFM segment** (#40) — RFM alone (recency +
  monetary) cannot find these 7,319 customers; behavioral clustering on delivery/
  satisfaction features is required.
- **At-Risk customers are 1.77x overrepresented in the Northeast** (16.68% of At-Risk
  customers vs. 9.42% of the overall customer base) (#41) — directly connecting the
  segmentation finding back to the geographic finding in 4.4.
- The segmentation is **highly stable**: mean bootstrap ARI = 0.979 across resamples
  (#42).
- Of 96,096 total customers, 92,746 (96.5%) have the complete order/review/delivery
  history required for segmentation; the excluded 3,350 are customers whose only
  orders lack a review or delivery outcome (e.g., canceled/unavailable) — documented,
  not silently dropped.

### 4.8 Predictive risk modeling
- **Model A (late-delivery risk, at order time)**: AUC=0.742, PR-AUC=0.215 vs. an
  8.19% base rate. Top per-feature predictors: `estimated_delivery_days` and
  `distance_km` (#44).
- **Flagging the riskiest 10% of orders (decile 1) at order time catches 31.9% of all
  eventual late deliveries** (2,472 of 7,758) — a **3.2x lift** over the 8.19%
  baseline (26.10% late rate in decile 1) (#46). Lift decays smoothly and
  monotonically through decile 10 (1.24% late rate) — the model is well-calibrated for
  prioritization, not just classification.
- Grouped feature importance for Model A surfaces `purchase_month` (52.6%) ahead of
  `estimated_delivery_days` (19.7%) and `distance_km` (13.2%) — driven almost entirely
  by a single month, March 2018, the epicenter of the Feb-Mar 2018 fulfillment
  anomaly. **This does not contradict #44** — it's a grouped (per-dimension) view vs.
  a per-feature view; both are correct and are presented side-by-side on the dashboard
  with an explanatory note (#45).
- **Model B (negative-review risk, post-delivery)**: AUC≈0.745, PR-AUC≈0.270 vs. a
  12.81% base rate. `delivery_delay_days` (39.4%) + `is_late` (32.5%) = **71.8%** of
  total feature importance — delivery experience overwhelmingly drives review
  sentiment (#47). `n_items` (12.2%) is a distant third — basket complexity matters,
  but only after delivery performance (#48).
- **Model B's decile 1 has a 56.0% negative-review rate** (4.4x the 12.81% baseline),
  capturing **43.8% of all negative reviews in just 10% of orders** (#49).
- **Model A's order-time risk score still predicts Model B's post-delivery outcome**
  (#50) — meaning a high-risk flag at checkout is itself an early warning for
  satisfaction risk, not just delivery risk. This is the analytical bridge between
  Recommendations 1 and 2 below.

## 6. Recommendations & Projected Impact

Each recommendation ties directly to one or more findings above. Impact estimates are
**illustrative Fermi estimates derived from this dataset's own observed rates** —
they show the *order of magnitude* an intervention could plausibly reach, with
explicit assumptions stated so they can be replaced with real numbers once an
intervention is piloted. None of these are claims of validated results; all are framed
as hypotheses worth testing (ideally via a holdout/A-B design).

### Recommendation 1 — Proactive delay communication for orders trending toward 4+ day delays
**Tied to:** §4.5 (the cliff), §4.8 (Model A/B linkage, #50)
**The opportunity:** Orders that end up 4+ days late are only **5.24%** of reviewed
deliveries but generate **~26% of all negative reviews** (~3,177 "excess" negatives
vs. the Early-bucket baseline rate). Because Model A's order-time score predicts
Model B's outcome (#50), these orders can often be identified *before* they're late.
**Proposed action:** for orders whose in-transit tracking signals indicate they are
falling into the 4+ day delay zone, trigger a proactive notification ("your order is
running a few days behind — here's your updated estimate") rather than letting the
customer discover the delay on arrival.
**Illustrative impact:** if proactive expectation-setting closes **15–30%** of the gap
between the Late 4-7/8+ blended negative rate (~67%) and the Early baseline (9.15%)
for the 5,025 affected orders, that's **~440–870 fewer negative reviews** over the
~21-month window (~21–41/month). At the lower bound this is conservative because it
assumes communication alone (no operational fix) changes the *outcome's perception*,
not the delay itself — which prior research on service-recovery and expectation-
setting suggests is plausible but should be validated with an A/B test before scaling.

### Recommendation 2 — Order-time risk triage using Model A's top decile
**Tied to:** §4.8 (#46, #49, #50)
**The opportunity:** Model A's riskiest decile (9,473 orders) has a 26.10% late rate
(3.2x baseline) and captures 31.9% of all late deliveries; those same orders carry
elevated Model-B risk post-delivery.
**Proposed action:** route decile-1 orders to an expedited carrier lane, an earlier
carrier handoff SLA, or at minimum a customer-facing tracking nudge — operationally
cheaper than treating all orders uniformly, since it targets 10% of volume.
**Illustrative impact:** a 25% relative reduction in decile-1's late rate
(26.10%→19.6%) converts **~493 orders/window from late to on-time**; a 50% relative
reduction converts **~988**. Each conversion removes that order from the Late-bucket
negative-review pool in Recommendation 1, so these two levers compound rather than
double-count their impact in practice — they're two different *trigger points*
(pre-emptive routing vs. reactive communication) for the same root cause.

### Recommendation 3 — Northeast-focused fulfillment investment
**Tied to:** §4.4, §4.7 (#25, #27, #28, #41)
**The opportunity:** the Northeast is 9.47% of orders but has a 14.33% late rate
(vs. 7.05–7.45% in the South/Southeast core) and the lowest regional avg review
(3.97★ vs. 4.18–4.19★). The SLA-buffer analysis (#25) shows Northeast orders are
promised the *thinnest* delivery windows despite needing more time — and At-Risk
customers are 1.77x overrepresented there.
**Proposed action:** evaluate a regional cross-dock/fulfillment partnership for
Northeast-bound orders (mirroring how the model already shows 64% of single-seller
orders are cross-state, #28) and recalibrate Northeast SLA estimates to reflect actual
transit times rather than a thinner buffer.
**Illustrative impact:** closing just half the gap between Northeast's 14.33% late
rate and the Southeast's 7.45% (i.e., reaching ~10.9%) across Northeast's 9,305 annual
order-equivalent volume implies **~320 fewer late Northeast orders/window** — which,
per Recommendation 1's rates, would itself prevent a meaningful share of the ~26%
delay-attributable negative reviews specifically in the region with the worst current
satisfaction score.

### Recommendation 4 — Win-back campaign for the "High-Value Lapsed" RFM segment
**Tied to:** §4.7 (#37)
**The opportunity:** 14,446 customers (15.6% of the base) historically contributed
**29.3% of revenue** (avg R$310.09/customer) but have an average recency of ~398 days
— they've gone quiet.
**Proposed action:** a targeted reactivation campaign (personalized offer referencing
past purchase categories, since `n_categories` is tracked per customer).
**Illustrative impact:** at a **5–10% reactivation rate** (typical range for e-commerce
win-back campaigns; should be validated against Olist's actual channel performance),
reactivating 722–1,445 customers at their historical avg order value of R$310 implies
**R$224K–R$448K in incremental GMV** from a single campaign — before accounting for
any subsequent repeat purchases.

### Recommendation 5 — Loyalty program targeting the "Loyal Repeat Customers" profile
**Tied to:** §4.7 (segment table)
**The opportunity:** this segment is only 2.5% of customers but has a **71.5%
repeat-purchase rate** (40x the rate of any other segment), plus the highest
installment usage (3.68) and category breadth (2.05). The defining inflection point is
the **2nd purchase**: this segment's avg frequency (1.85) is the only one materially
above 1.0.
**Proposed action:** (a) a loyalty/VIP tier with perks (free shipping thresholds,
early access) targeted at customers approaching their 2nd purchase, and (b) as future
work, a propensity model trained to identify "future Loyal Repeat" customers after
their 1st order, enabling proactive nurturing rather than waiting for the 2nd purchase
to happen organically.
**Why this matters disproportionately:** a segment this small driving 40x the repeat
rate of everyone else suggests there's an identifiable behavioral signature (multi-
category, installment-financed purchases) that, if encouraged earlier in the
lifecycle, could shift marginal customers from "Budget Satisfied" (45.1% of the base,
0.90% repeat rate) toward this profile.

### Recommendation 6 — Seller-performance program addressing marketplace concentration risk
**Tied to:** §4.3 (#29), Page 6 (seller Pareto)
**The opportunity:** the top 10% of sellers (306 of 3,053) generate **67.5% of
marketplace revenue** — a concentration that is both a single-point-of-failure risk
(losing a handful of top sellers materially impacts GMV) and, for the long tail of
high-`pct_late`/low-review sellers, a quality-control gap that compounds the
geographic issues in Recommendation 3.
**Proposed action:** (a) seller-diversification incentives (onboarding/promotion
support for sellers outside the top decile, especially in underrepresented regions),
and (b) seller-scorecard-triggered performance-improvement plans for sellers whose
`pct_late`/`avg_review_score` fall below thresholds, prioritized where seller region
overlaps with the Northeast finding from Recommendation 3.

### Recommendation 7 — Category-level product/expectation audits, independent of logistics
**Tied to:** §4.6 (#33)
**The opportunity:** 16 of 53 categories have review scores significantly different
from the grand mean **independent of delivery performance** — e.g.
`office_furniture` (3.51★) vs. `books_general_interest` (4.51★). A delivery-focused
intervention (Recommendations 1–3) will not move these.
**Proposed action:** product-page and packaging audits for the bottom-quartile
categories on this dimension — description accuracy, sizing/fit expectations
(especially for furniture/apparel-adjacent categories), and packaging-damage rates.

### Recommendation 8 — Seasonal capacity planning ahead of November demand spikes
**Tied to:** §4.2 (#18, #22), §4.4 (Feb-Mar 2018 anomaly, Page 4)
**The opportunity:** Nov-2017 GMV was +64% vs. trailing average — a clear, recurring
seasonal surge. The Feb-Mar 2018 fulfillment crisis (the dominant signal in Model A's
`purchase_month` feature, #45) shows what happens when capacity doesn't anticipate a
demand/operational shift.
**Proposed action:** use the Holt-Winters forecast band (#22) to proactively scale
carrier capacity and warehouse staffing ahead of known seasonal peaks, treating Feb-Mar
2018 as a cautionary case study for what *unplanned* capacity shortfalls do to the
late-rate distribution shown on Page 4.

## 7. Limitations & Future Work

- **Dataset window** is Sep 2016 – Sep 2018; recommendations are based on historical
  patterns and should be validated against current operations before acting on them.
- **Impact estimates in Section 6 are illustrative**, derived from this dataset's own
  observed rates under stated assumptions — not validated experimental results. Each
  should be piloted with a holdout/control group before full rollout.
- **Model PR-AUC (0.215 / 0.270)** reflects genuinely hard prediction problems at
  ~8-13% base rates — both models are well-suited to **prioritization/triage** (decile
  lift is strong and monotonic) but not to precise individual-order guarantees.
  Periodic retraining is recommended as new data accrues.
- **The World Cup hypothesis (#21)** for the Jun-2018 GMV dip is plausible but
  unverified — would require external benchmark data to confirm.
- **Geographic analysis is state-level**; #30 shows city-level concentration is
  sharper, so Recommendations 3 and 6 would sharpen further with city-level targeting.
- **Segmentation excludes 3,350 customers (3.5%)** whose only orders lack a review or
  delivery outcome — documented and immaterial to the segment-level conclusions, but
  worth tracking if their share grows.
